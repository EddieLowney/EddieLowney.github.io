Impact of Weather and Road Conditions on Vehicle Crashes in Montogomery County, Maryland

Introduction

Montgomery county is the most popular county in Maryland with over 1 million residents. Montgomery also has the second highest cost of living in the state and is a broadly wealthy population. It is also a popular county to drive through in order to get to the nation’s capital: Washington, D.C as well as Baltimore. Because of this increased traffic, and its large population, Montgomery county serves as an excellent candidate for analysis of traffic incidents which is generalizable to both communters, residents taking shorter trips, and long-haul traffic. Our project aims to analyze the impact of weather and road conditions in car crashes and how they impact the wide-ranging levels in severity of crashes in Montgomery county. Specifically, we hope to determine if there is a strong up-tick in crash severity due to weather conditions, if there are specific geographic areas or roads which are especially prone to severe accidents, and whether other factors like vehicle age and road speed limits are relevant. This interactive map shows where crashes happened in the 2024 calendar year. Although many points are shown at default, users can filter crashes based on the type of injury sustained as a result of the crash: serious, minor, and possible injury. As a result, these different types of injuries are also represented with a different color. By filtering for only crashes leading to serious injuries, we see that a majority of these crashes happen on major highways and roads, instead of residential areas. In addition, it seems like the weather conditions do not seem to have an affect on the severity of these crashes whatsoever because some of these crashes happened in clear weather. On the map, this is represented by solid color lines like pink and yellow. Some recurring roads that have had serious injuries take place include Georgia Avenue, Frederick Road, and Eisenhower Memorial Highway. It makes sense that more serious injuries would happen on these types of roads, but it’s important to acknowledge this to further investigate what can be done on the roads to minimize the number of crashes that lead to fatal or serious injuries.

This side by side bar plot displays the proportion of crashes that occur in Montgomery County based on the weather hazard type and the injury severity sustained by the driver. Since there are so many different weather types in this dataset, we decided to split the types fitted into three categories: Not Hazardous, Moderate Hazardous, and Very Hazardous. We decided to use a proportion rather than count to determine if more serious injuries would increase as the weather gets more dangerous for drivers. We see that there is no difference in the proportion of major injuries when the driver is not at fault. However, this proportion slightly increases when the driver is responsible for the crash. For possible injuries, it increases by 1.5% from not hazardous to very hazardous. Additionally, minor injuries increase by 0.6% from not hazardous to moderate hazardous in this category. This suggests that there are many more factors to consider that impact the condition of the driver other than how the weather is. Some possibilities include how visible the road conditions were or how fast the driver was going.

This is a stacked area chart that displays the evolution of multiple groups' values on the same graphic - visualizing the proportion of crash severities on each date during clear weather conditions over time (from early 2024 to March 2025). Each color represents a different severity level, and the vertical axis shows the proportion (%) of crashes on that specific day. This graph focuses mostly on clear weather, as it has the most occurrences of car crashes in the past year. The x-axis represents a time series of clear weather conditions from early 2024 to March 2025, while the y-axis portrays the percentage of crashes (0-100%) attributed to each severity level on a given day. The legend on the top right shows the 5 common severity levels: fatal injury (very rare), no apparent injury (dominates the graph), possible injury, suspected minor injury, and suspected serious injury (not as common as minor injury but more common than fatal). Possible injury represents data that have not yet been classified as minor or serious injuries but are most likely under one of those categories.

This heatmap depicts the proportion of accidents which resulted in major injury or fatality for different weather types (displayed on the y-axis) and vehicle age (displayed on the x-axis). This plot demonstrates a significantly greater risk for individuals driving older vehicles to sustain significant injury in the event of a crash. Perhaps counterintuitively, this graph also suggests that older cars do not see a decrease in safety relative to newer cars in more extreme weather, like snow and rain. The data suggests that, instead, the difference in major injury likelihood between newer and older cars decreases.

Conclusion

These visualizations demonstrate a somewhat minor correlation between environmental factors — weather and road conditions — and crash occurrence and severity. However, the differences in severity we observed were small enough that it is doubtful that weather plays a particularly outsized role. Despite this, these insights are still important for drivers. Drivers in Montgomery County, as well as road safety officers, may be enabled to better direct their resources towards dealing with other factors, such as speeding and ensuring vehicle fitness for the road, which our later analysis showed to be perhaps more strongly linked to accident severity. Overall, our results can be support targeted safety campaigns, smarter infrastructure planning, and the potential development of real-time interventions, such as weather-triggered traffic alerts. Our analysis of particularly dangerous roads could also be helpful for safety purposes. In summary, while we failed to identify strong correlations which pinpoint a possible cause for major accidents, our analysis still provides additional data which can inform better safety measures.
